Creating a Personal Portfolio Website: Showcasing Skills and Growth

Introduction & Context

The personal portfolio website project was a self-initiated endeavor aimed at creating a professional online presence to showcase my skills, projects, and experiences. The goal was to provide a dynamic and visually appealing platform to highlight my expertise in project management, data analysis, and web development.

This project was entirely self-driven, involving extensive learning, experimentation, and iteration to achieve a high-quality result. Feedback from friends and family was solicited after creating a beta version, which ultimately led to a complete redesign and rebuild of the project.

  • Professional Showcasing: Display my projects, skills, and resume in a polished, interactive way.
  • User Experience: Ensure that the website is easy to navigate and engaging for visitors, particularly recruiters or potential collaborators.
  • Learning Opportunity: Use this project as a hands-on opportunity to improve my web development skills and explore new design trends and tools.

The plan was divided into phases, starting with brainstorming and prototyping, moving into development, and concluding with feedback-based iterations.

Design

The design phase involved extensive ideation and prototyping:

  • Initial Prototypes: I used CSS tutorials and training courses to create the initial layouts, focusing on a modern, minimalist design to ensure a professional look and feel.
  • Key Features:
    A dynamic home page with an introduction and quick navigation.
    A portfolio section showcasing projects with detailed write-ups and visuals.
    A skills and resume section with interactive elements to highlight my expertise.
    A contact form and links to my contact info and social media.
  • Visual Identity: I chose a sleek color palette, professional typography, and a responsive layout to cater to both desktop and mobile users.
Develop

The development phase involved translating the design into a fully functional website:

Test

After gathering feedback on the beta version, it became clear that improvements were needed to better reflect my skills and professional goals. Key feedback included:

  • Simplifying navigation for easier access to information.

Based on this feedback, I decided to start over and rebuild the project from scratch, focusing on:

Expectations

The expectations for the final version of the website are:

  • Professional Appeal: A polished platform that effectively represents my personal brand.
  • Ease of Updates: A structure that allows for easy updates as I gain new skills or complete new projects.
  • Learning Outcomes: Improved proficiency in web development tools and frameworks, including advanced front-end techniques and SEO optimization.
My Role

As the sole creator of the project, my responsibilities included:

WMS Training: From Data Analysis to Web Development

Introduction & Context

The Warehouse Management System (WMS) Training project aimed to design and implement a robust training program for employees operating WMS software in logistics environments. With the increasing digitization of warehouse operations, understanding how to effectively use WMS platforms has become critical for ensuring efficiency and accuracy in inventory management, order fulfillment, and overall supply chain operations.

The project was initiated as part of my role in data analysis and web project managment at the logistics firm Bleckmann seeking to upskill its workforce and streamline its warehouse processes. The focus was to create an interactive training program tailored to the company's WMS software, targeting both novice and experienced employees. This initiative required balancing technical complexities with accessibility to ensure widespread adoption and engagement.

Plan

The planning phase began by identifying the key objectives:

  • Understand the current WMS software's functionalities and limitations.
  • Analyze the knowledge gaps among employees and their pain points.
  • Develop a training program that includes theoretical knowledge, hands-on practice, and real-world scenarios.

To achieve these goals, we divided the project into phases:

  • Desk research on WMS software and best practices in training delivery.
  • Field research to collect employee feedback and insights through surveys and interviews.
  • Iterative design and testing of the training program with real-time feedback from participants.

Potential risks included resistance to change from employees, variability in skill levels, and technological constraints such as outdated hardware.

Design

The training program was structured around three core pillars:

  • Introduction to WMS: Covering basic concepts, navigation, and system functionalities.
  • Process-Specific Modules: Training employees on tasks such as inventory tracking, order picking, and report generation.
  • Advanced Features and Troubleshooting: Equipping experienced users with skills to handle complex scenarios and system errors.

Key design elements included:

  • Interactive Tutorials: Guided walkthroughs of system functionalities.
  • Gamification: Quizzes and challenges to enhance engagement.
  • Scenario-Based Simulations: Real-life scenarios to test problem-solving skills.

The content was tailored to various user groups based on their roles, from warehouse operators to managers.

Development

During development, we created detailed training materials, including:

  • Step-by-step guides with visual aids.
  • Video tutorials demonstrating system workflows.
  • A sandbox environment where employees could practice without impacting live operations; using html, css and JavaScript.

We collaborated closely with the company’s IT team to ensure compatibility with their existing WMS software. Additionally, we integrated feedback from focus groups into the development process, ensuring the training addressed specific user needs.

Testing

The training program was piloted with a small group of employees representing different roles within the warehouse. This testing phase helped us:

  • Identify gaps in the training content.
  • Observe how employees interacted with the materials and the software.
  • Collect feedback on the clarity, usability, and effectiveness of the program.

Key insights from the testing phase included the need for:

  • More simplified instructions for first-time users.
  • Additional practice sessions for complex workflows.
  • Live support during training to address immediate questions.
Review

The review phase focused on refining the training program based on feedback from the pilot. Adjustments included:

  • Simplifying technical jargon in the materials.
  • Incorporating more visual aids to support textual content.
  • Adding a feedback loop within the training modules to continuously improve their relevance and clarity.

The review also highlighted the importance of ongoing support for employees post-training, leading to the development of a resource hub with FAQs and troubleshooting guides.

Agile Methodology in Action

The project followed an Agile methodology to ensure flexibility and adaptability.

  • Sprints were organized around key milestones: content creation, pilot testing, and final delivery.
  • Monday.com was used for task tracking and to manage dependencies between the project management and operational teams.
  • Weekly stand-up meetings via Microsoft Teams facilitated communication and progress updates.
  • Regular feedback sessions with the company’s HR and operations teams allowed for continuous improvement.

The final training program will be deployed across the company’s warehouse network, with sessions conducted both in-person and virtually to accommodate varying schedules and locations. The implementation included:

  • Onboarding sessions for new employees.
  • Refresher courses for experienced users.
  • A certification process to ensure participants met proficiency standards.

Employee engagement was tracked through participation rates, quiz scores, and post-training surveys. Results showed high satisfaction levels and increased confidence in using the WMS software.

Outcomes

The WMS training program resulted in significant operational benefits:

  • Increased Efficiency: Employees demonstrated improved speed and accuracy in using the WMS.
  • Reduced Errors: Enhanced understanding of the system minimized common mistakes in inventory management and order processing.
  • Employee Satisfaction: Participants reported greater confidence and satisfaction in their roles, fostering a positive workplace culture.

The organization also observed measurable improvements in key performance indicators, including faster order fulfillment times and fewer delays caused by system errors.

My Role

In my role as project manager and data analyst, I contributed in the following ways:

  • Project Management: Coordinating timelines, resources, and team efforts to ensure successful project completion.
  • Data Analysis: Using system usage metrics and employee feedback to identify pain points and measure program impact.
  • Content Development: Collaborating with the project management and operational teams to create materials tailored to employee needs.
  • Stakeholder Communication: Providing regular updates to management and incorporating their input into project planning and execution.

This project reinforced my skills in logistics, data-driven decision-making, and team leadership, demonstrating the value of structured training programs in driving organizational success.

Performance Insights: A Comprehensive Business Review Dashboard

Introduction & Context

The Business Reviews Project aimed to streamline and enhance how performance data was presented to key stakeholders within the company. The primary deliverable was a user-friendly dashboard that consolidated complex business metrics into an intuitive, visually appealing, and actionable interface.

The project was initiated to address inefficiencies in traditional reporting methods, which relied on static presentations and dense spreadsheets. These formats were time-consuming to interpret and did not support real-time data exploration, creating a barrier to informed decision-making.

My role in this project focused on UX design, data analysis, and ensuring the dashboard presented accurate, actionable data by leveraging Tableau and SQL.

Plan

The planning phase revolved around understanding stakeholder needs and defining project objectives. Key milestones included:

  • Stakeholder Interviews: Conducting interviews with decision-makers and end-users to identify pain points in current reporting methods.
  • Defining Dashboard Requirements: Outlining the key metrics, data sources, and desired functionality for the dashboard.
  • Improving Data Accuracy: Identifying inconsistencies in existing data pipelines and setting up protocols for cleaner data extraction and transformation using Tableau, WMS and Excel.
  • Establishing Design Principles: Prioritizing clarity, usability, and accessibility to ensure the dashboard catered to a diverse user base.

Risk management strategies were also developed to address potential challenges, such as data integration issues or resistance to adopting a new tool.

Key features of the design included:

Development

  • Data Validation: Conducting thorough checks to ensure data accuracy across multiple sources.

  • Performance Testing: Ensuring the dashboard loaded quickly and handled large datasets without lag.

Testing revealed several areas for improvement, such as simplifying navigation menus, adding tooltips to clarify data points, and fine-tuning SQL queries for faster data retrieval.

The review process focused on evaluating the dashboard’s effectiveness in meeting project objectives. Metrics used for evaluation included:

The review confirmed that the dashboard not only improved reporting efficiency but also significantly enhanced trust in the data presented.

    Introducing a Project Management Tool

    Introduction & Context

    Effective project management is essential in any logistics firm to streamline workflows, enhance communication, and meet client expectations. Initially, we faced challenges with fragmented processes reliant on Excel sheets and an overwhelming number of emails, which resulted in inefficiencies and delays. Addressing these bottlenecks required introducing a structured project management tool that could centralize operations, improve visibility, and adapt to the needs of different teams.

    Plan

    The project began with identifying the existing challenges across the inbound, customer service, customs, and operations teams. The objectives were clear:

    • Reduce unnecessary email communication.
    • Create a centralized system for task tracking and progress updates.
    • Improve team collaboration and provide clear client-specific overviews.

    The stakeholders included:

    • Inbound and Customer Service Teams: Focused on daily task coordination.
    • Customs and Operations Supervisors: Needed high-level overviews and reporting capabilities.
    • Project Management Team: Responsible for overseeing the overall implementation and ensuring alignment with company goals.
    Design

    The initial step involved mapping out workflows and identifying core requirements for the tool. Early designs included:

    • Creating task boards to visualize progress.
    • Customizing statuses for various client needs.
    • Setting up different views for operational tasks, returns, and B2B processes.

    This phase also included presentations to stakeholders, explaining the planned changes and gathering their feedback to ensure alignment with team-specific needs.

    Develop

    The development process was iterative, starting with implementing Trello as a prototype to test the Kanban methodology. Initial boards were created with:

    • Standardized workflows for inbound and customer service teams.
    • Custom statuses for specific clients to improve clarity.
    • Separate boards for B2B tasks and returns processing.

    Trello’s simplicity allowed the teams to adapt quickly and test the new system without significant disruptions. However, its limitations in client-specific dashboards and advanced calendar views prompted further refinements.

    Test

    Testing the Trello setup involved:

    • Daily stand-ups with the inbound and customer service teams to gather feedback and identify gaps.
    • Weekly reviews with customs, operational supervisors, and project management to assess its scalability.

    Issues identified included:

    • Difficulty in tracking client-specific data in one place.
    • Limited calendar functionality for scheduling tasks and deadlines.

    These tests highlighted the need for a more robust solution, setting the stage for further tool exploration.

    Review

    The review phase was pivotal in determining the long-term tool that best suited the organization’s needs. While Trello addressed initial inefficiencies, its limitations prompted a reassessment of options. Key steps included:

    • Tool Comparison: Evaluated several project management tools, such as Asana, Wrike, and Monday.com, focusing on scalability, customization, and integration capabilities.
    • Stakeholder Feedback: Gathered evolving requirements from all teams to ensure the tool met operational needs.
    • Cost-Benefit Analysis: Analyzed ROI based on productivity gains and implementation costs, leading to the decision to adopt Monday.com.
    • Risk Mitigation: Developed a migration plan to ensure a smooth transition, minimizing workflow disruptions.
    Agile Method Follow-Up

    The project followed Agile principles to adapt to continuous feedback and evolving requirements:

    • Kanban Boards: Initially used Trello boards as the foundation for workflows.
    • Sprint Meetings: Conducted daily stand-ups with inbound and customer service teams, and weekly updates with customs and operational supervisors.
    • Incremental Improvements: Adjusted workflows and dashboards iteratively based on feedback.

    The transition to Monday.com involved:

    • Data Migration: Transferring data from Trello and Excel to Monday.com with minimal downtime.
    • Customization: Creating dashboards with advanced visuals for better task tracking and client-specific overviews.
    • Training: Conducting workshops to familiarize teams with the new tool, emphasizing its benefits and features.

    Daily sprint meetings on Microsoft Teams ensured smooth coordination, and a Trello-based Kanban board tracked the transition’s progress.

    Outcomes

    The introduction of Monday.com significantly improved:

    • Efficiency: Reduced email communication by over 12 mails per day.
    • Visibility: Enhanced task tracking with customizable dashboards.
    • Client Experience: Provided better client-specific overviews and seamless scheduling with calendar integration.

    Teams adapted quickly to the new tool, and continuous feedback ensured its ongoing success.

    My Role

    My primary responsibilities included:

    • Data Analysis: Identified inefficiencies and outlined requirements for the new system.
    • Presentations & Convincing: Delivered compelling presentations to management, emphasizing the benefits of transitioning to Trello and later to Monday.com.
    • Tool Comparison & Cost Analysis: Compared potential tools and performed a detailed ROI analysis to justify the transition to Monday.com.
    • Implementation Lead: Managed data migration, setup, and customization of Monday.com.
    • Training & Support: Conducted workshops and provided ongoing support to ensure teams maximized the tool’s potential.
    • Stakeholder Collaboration: Coordinated with all teams, consolidating their feedback to refine the system iteratively.

    Through this project, I honed my skills in project management, data analysis, and stakeholder engagement, driving efficiency and collaboration within the organization.

    Start2Found: Bridging Founders and Starters Through a Swipe App

    Introduction & Context

    Start2Found was a student-led entrepreneurial initiative aimed at creating a platform that connects startup founders with individuals eager to join and contribute to entrepreneurial ventures. The concept emerged from a recognized gap where founders struggled to find collaborators with the right skill sets and starters lacked opportunities to contribute their expertise to promising ideas.

    The project involved collaboration between three students, each bringing unique expertise: app development, SME management, and project management. Stakeholders included expertise centers like "Expedition DO!" and "Gentrepreneur," as well as potential users—entrepreneurs and skilled individuals eager to start their journey.

    Plan

    The idea for Start2Found was inspired by a personal conversation with a friend who shared his challenges in launching startup companies. Despite having creative and innovative ideas, his ventures struggled due to a lack of collaborators with complementary skill sets. This inspired the creation of a platform to address this problem.

    The core plan revolved around building a swipe-based app that allowed founders to pitch their startup ideas while showcasing their required skill sets. Starters, in turn, could present their expertise, creating an ecosystem of mutual benefit and collaboration.

    Design

    The application’s design mimicked the functionality of popular swipe-based platforms. The dual user base consisted of:

    • Founders: Individuals with startup ideas. They could create profiles with brief pitches of their ideas, indicating the skill sets they were looking for in collaborators.
    • Starters: Individuals eager to join startups but lacking ideas of their own. Their profiles showcased their skills and the roles they were seeking in startup ventures.

    As Product Manager, I worked closely with the app developer to define how the SaaS platform should function. This included mapping user flows, conceptualizing an intuitive user interface, and incorporating features that aligned with user needs, such as a swipe-to-connect mechanism and detailed profile customization options.

    Develop

    Development began by leveraging competitions like "Gentrepreneur" and "Expedition DO!" These platforms allowed the team to pitch the app concept, receive valuable feedback from industry experts, and network with potential users. The competitions also provided critical insights into user needs and market positioning.

    Marketing efforts included creating initial branding elements, such as logos and app design using Figma. SEO and SEA strategies were researched, along with copyrighting, to lay the foundation for future scalability.

    Test

    Feedback sessions at pitching events proved instrumental in refining the concept. The team engaged directly with target users—students and young professionals keen on entrepreneurship—and used their input to iterate on the app design and functionality. The dual feedback loop from expertise centers and users enabled continuous improvements to the app's core proposition.

    Review

    During the review phase, it became apparent that the main target audience—students and aspiring entrepreneurs—was concentrated in educational institutions. To maximize reach, the team needed access to entrepreneurship-focused schools, such as Artevelde University College and Ghent University, for in-class presentations and targeted outreach.

    However, as team members transitioned into full-time jobs and pursued other commitments, the decision was made to discontinue the project.

    Agile Method Follow-Up

    The team implemented Agile methodologies to manage the project.

    • Daily meetings: These facilitated real-time communication and progress tracking.
    • Collaboration tools: Microsoft Teams and Trello were used for task assignments, timeline tracking, and sharing updates.

    This approach enabled the team to adapt quickly to challenges and incorporate stakeholder feedback into the development process.

    Start2Found was developed through iterative cycles of pitching, feedback incorporation, and design improvements. While the app did not progress to full deployment, the project validated the need for such a platform and highlighted critical design and marketing elements that could inform future iterations.

    Outcomes

    The project successfully achieved several milestones, including:

    • Establishing a working prototype and branding for Start2Found.

    CSR Training for Women in Leadership Positions

    Introduction & Context

    The CSR Training for Women in Leadership Positions was part of my bachelor’s thesis project, conducted with a team of five students from Artevelde University of Applied Sciences in the Business Management - International Entrepreneurship program. Our client, Veronica Rubio, is a Co-Founder of Etika and Vectra International. Etika specializes in Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) training, focusing on sustainability strategies, social audits, and grievance mechanisms. Their mission is to simplify CSR practices and empower women globally.

    The objective was to explore the potential for expanding Etika's CSR training program into four countries—Belgium, Portugal, South Korea, and Spain—while catering to different target groups: students, managers, and companies.

    This project also aligned with the emerging trends of sustainability and gender equality, with CSR and women in leadership positions being increasingly important for companies seeking to improve their societal and environmental impact.

    Plan

    To kickstart the project, we outlined the research objectives based on the client’s needs:

    • Gauge interest in CSR training across the four countries.
    • Identify cultural and economic differences influencing training implementation.
    • Determine marketing strategies for B2C and B2B audiences.
    • Analyze gender-specific interest in CSR training, with a focus on women in leadership.

    The project involved desk research into CSR practices, market analysis, and competitors, combined with field research through surveys and interviews. A significant challenge was the COVID-19 pandemic, which restricted in-person interactions, requiring virtual meetings and interviews.

    Design

    We began by analyzing Veronica Rubio’s companies and projects, focusing on Etika. A thorough examination revealed:

    • CSR training primarily appeals to women aiming for leadership roles.
    • Competitors often lacked a personal approach, an area where Etika could differentiate itself.
    • Language barriers and cultural sensitivities required tailoring training programs for each country.

    Our research questions were categorized to address specific needs:

    • Target audience analysis: What motivates students, managers, and companies to engage in CSR training?
    • Cultural impact: How do cultural and economic differences influence training adoption?
    • Marketing strategy: What is the most effective way to market the training program?
    • Gender focus: Why are women more interested in CSR training, and how can this be leveraged?
    Development

    The project moved forward by conducting surveys and interviews across the four countries, with a deep dive into Belgium and South Korea. These activities provided valuable insights:

    • Students expressed interest in CSR training but cited cost as a barrier, especially immediately after completing their studies.
    • Managers demonstrated enthusiasm for CSR training but preferred courses in their native language for better accessibility.
    • Companies showed a willingness to invest in training, particularly those already aligned with sustainability goals.

    To validate our findings and immerse ourselves in the topic, our team members also took one of Etika’s training courses, gaining firsthand experience.

    Testing

    The initial findings were shared with Veronica Rubio and iteratively refined through feedback. For instance:

    • The cost of the training program was seen as a significant barrier for students, leading us to explore potential subsidies or discounts.
    • Managers requested more industry-specific examples, guiding us to recommend sector-focused modules.

    Due to logistical and cultural challenges, we conducted virtual focus groups and surveys instead of in-person testing.

    Review

    The review phase allowed us to address stakeholder concerns and refine our recommendations. Some key areas of improvement included:

    • Enhancing Etika’s online presence through better SEO, SEA, and social media strategies.
    • Incorporating trackable metrics to measure training effectiveness for companies.
    • Developing multilingual content to cater to diverse audiences.
    Agile Methodology in Action

    The project was structured using Agile principles, with the team working in sprints to achieve incremental goals. Trello was used for task management in a Kanban format, ensuring transparency and collaboration. Weekly meetings with stakeholders, including Veronica Rubio, ensured constant feedback and alignment. Each sprint addressed a specific aspect of the project, such as market analysis, cultural research, and survey deployment.

    Execution involved applying insights from both desk and field research to create actionable recommendations for Etika’s CSR training program. This included:

    • A marketing strategy tailored to students, managers, and companies, with a focus on B2C and B2B dynamics.
    • Suggestions for pricing adjustments and localization of content.
    • A comprehensive competitor analysis highlighting opportunities for differentiation.
    Outcomes

    The project delivered a clear roadmap for Etika’s international expansion, addressing:

    • Market potential in the four countries.
    • Cultural and economic factors affecting adoption.
    • Gender-specific marketing strategies.

    Veronica Rubio and the stakeholders appreciated the thoroughness of the research and the actionable recommendations provided. The project demonstrated the feasibility of scaling CSR training internationally while maintaining its mission to empower women.

    • Organizing and leading weekly and daily meetings.
    • Managing the Agile process using Trello and Kanban boards.

    Xoombike: A Sustainable Bike Train for Kids

    Introduction & Context

    Xoombike is an innovative bike train concept designed to address multiple modern challenges: providing children with exercise, promoting sustainability, and reducing parental stress in getting kids to school. The bike train consists of a battery-driven go-cart at the front, capable of pulling several attached carts to form a train. This design ensures children can travel safely and sustainably, while also fostering physical activity.

    The Xoombike also includes integrated software features, such as a track-and-trace system for safety, calendar functionalities for efficient scheduling, and Uber-style pick-up point customization. These features make Xoombike an appealing solution for schools and parents alike, offering a cost-effective and environmentally friendly alternative to traditional school transportation.

    Plan

    The project began with defining our goals, guided by the subject “Emission Free Vehicles." Initial brainstorming focused on electric vehicles like cars, bikes, and scooters. However, we soon realized that these solutions lacked a clear target audience. By analyzing urban and suburban challenges, we identified parents as a key demographic, particularly those in smaller cities without access to sustainable transportation options.

    Parents, especially those juggling work and school drop-offs, faced significant stress in traffic. Addressing this gap became the foundation of our project—to create a solution that eased their burden while aligning with sustainability goals.

    Design

    With our target audience defined, we conceptualized a bike train that could transport children to school while reducing parental stress. Key design goals included:

    • Affordability: Offering a low-cost alternative to expensive options like electric school buses.
    • Sustainability: Utilizing battery-powered technology to minimize emissions.
    • Physical Activity: Encouraging children to engage in exercise during their commute.

    The initial design focused on creating a modular system—a battery-powered go-cart with detachable carts to accommodate varying numbers of children. This modularity ensured scalability and cost-effectiveness for schools and communities.

    Development

    The first phase of development involved constructing a prototype Xoombike. This prototype combined durability, ease of use, and safety considerations. The bike train’s design ensured flexibility in the number of children it could transport, while maintaining a compact and user-friendly structure.

    Testing

    We introduced our prototype to schools and parents, seeking feedback to refine the design. While the response was overwhelmingly positive, concerns about safety and tracking emerged. Some parents also expressed willingness to volunteer as drivers, which significantly reduced the projected operational costs.

    Review

    To address safety concerns, we conducted rigorous testing of the Xoombike’s structural integrity. Additional safety measures, such as helmets and fluorescent vests, were incorporated. For added peace of mind, we integrated SaaS and AI solutions:

    • Track-and-Trace: Allowing parents to monitor the Xoombike’s location in real time.
    • Scheduling Tools: Enabling schools to optimize pick-up and drop-off times.
    • Custom Pick-Up Points: Letting parents designate convenient locations for their children, similar to ride-sharing apps.

    These enhancements not only improved the product’s safety and usability but also increased trust and buy-in from stakeholders.

    Agile Methodology in Action

    Our development process was guided by Agile principles, emphasizing collaboration, flexibility, and iterative improvement. Key aspects of our workflow included:

    • Planning and Tools: We used Trello as our project management tool, organizing tasks in a Kanban format for transparency and efficiency.
    • Daily Stand-Ups: Held via Microsoft Teams, these meetings provided updates on individual and team progress, ensuring alignment and identifying blockers.
    • Stakeholder Engegament: Weekly presentations to our primary stakeholders (teachers) kept them informed and allowed for feedback. Monthly updates to parents and schools ensured our design and features aligned with their needs.

    The project evolved through iterative sprints, with each sprint focusing on a specific aspect—planning, design, prototyping, and testing. The collaborative and iterative nature of our process ensured continuous improvement and adaptability.

    Outcomes

    The final Xoombike prototype and its accompanying software received high praise from stakeholders, particularly for its innovative design and problem-solving approach. While the project concluded at the prototype stage, it demonstrated the viability of the concept and the effectiveness of Agile methodologies in project management.

    My Role

    As the team leader, I:

    • Guided the planning and goal-setting process.
    • Facilitated meetings and ensured effective communication among team members.
    • Managed stakeholder presentations and feedback sessions.
    • Oversaw the integration of all components into a coherent project, ensuring alignment with our objectives and stakeholder expectations.

    The Xoombike project exemplifies how sustainability, innovation, and user-centric design can come together to create impactful solutions.
